Gym Training Plan · Mid-30s Male

MountainAthlete

Backcountry SkiMountain Bike Hiking / TrailBackpacking Power Yoga ×1/wk1–2× Gym / Week

Design logic: Your yoga covers flexibility, balance, and body awareness. Your sports demand explosive leg power, single-leg stability, posterior chain strength, rotational core, and the pulling/carrying capacity for loaded packs. Desk work means you likely have tight hips and weak posterior chain from sitting — that's the corrective thread woven through both sessions. Each day stands alone for one-session weeks, but they complement each other when you get both in.

The Sessions
A
Legs, Power & Core
Priority session · Do this when you only get one day
Warm-Up · ~8 min
Hip 90/90 Stretch + Transitionsexample
2 min
bodyweight
Corrects hip tightness from sitting; directly carries into ski hip mobility
Sit on the floor, both legs bent at 90° — front shin parallel to you, back shin out to the side
Keep your spine tall; don't round forward to chase the stretch
Transition by lifting both knees simultaneously and switching sides — don't use your hands to push off
Lean forward over the front shin to increase depth — stop when hips start to rotate
Glute Bridge + Leg Curlexample
2×10
bodyweight
Activates posterior chain before loading; hamstring work critical for ski safety
Feet flat on floor, hip-width apart, knees at ~90°
Drive through heels — not toes — to lift your hips. Squeeze glutes at the top
Don't hyperextend your lower back at the top; ribs stay down
For the curl variation, use a stability ball or slider under your feet and curl heels toward glutes at the top of the bridge
Lateral Band Walksexample
2×15 ea
band
Hip abductor activation; keeps knees tracking on hard ski turns
Band around ankles or just above knees — ankles is harder
Slight squat stance (quarter squat), chest up, tension in band at all times
Step out wide enough to keep band taut; don't let feet come fully together
Move from the hip, not the knee — don't let knees cave inward as you step
World's Greatest Stretchexample
4 ea side
bodyweight
Total mobility primer — thoracic, hip flexor, hamstring in one movement
Start in a lunge: step forward, back knee down. Plant same-side hand on the floor inside your front foot
Rotate the opposite arm up toward the ceiling, following your hand with your eyes
Drive your front knee out slightly to open the hip — don't let it cave in
Move slowly and breathe through each rep; this is mobility, not a race
Main Work · ~35 min
Barbell Back Squat or Goblet Squat
4×5–6
lbs
Primary ski strength movement. Go heavy. Goblet if lower back is fatigued
Bar sits on upper traps (high bar) or rear delts (low bar). Squeeze the bar hard — creates upper back tension
Feet shoulder-width, toes slightly out (10–30°). Find YOUR stance — not everyone is the same
Brace your core like you're about to take a punch before you unrack. Maintain that brace the whole rep
Break at hips and knees simultaneously. Knees track over toes — push them out
Hit at least parallel (hip crease below knee). Drive floor away on the way up — don't think "up," think "push"
Goblet version: hold dumbbell or kettlebell at chest, elbows inside knees at the bottom
Romanian Deadlift (RDL)example
3×8
lbs
Posterior chain — glutes, hamstrings, lower back. Protects knees on descent
Stand with bar or dumbbells against your thighs. Shoulders back and down, slight bend in knees — lock that knee angle
Hinge at the hips by pushing them BACK, not bending at the waist. Think: "close a car door with your hips"
Bar/dumbbells drag down your legs the whole time — never leave your body
Lower until you feel a hard pull in your hamstrings (typically shins level or just below). Don't go past your mobility
Drive hips forward to stand — squeeze glutes hard at the top
Bulgarian Split Squatexample
3×8 ea
lbs
Single-leg power = real ski terrain. Reveals and corrects side-to-side imbalances
Rear foot elevated on a bench (laces down) or a box. Front foot far enough forward that your front shin stays vertical
Lower straight down — don't let your torso pitch excessively forward
Front knee tracks over your toes. Don't let it cave inward — this is a ski-specific cue
Back knee descends toward — but doesn't slam — the floor
Drive through your front heel to stand. Weaker side first, every set
Step-Up with Knee Drive (weighted)example
3×10 ea
lbs
Mimics uphill hiking step pattern. Use a 16–20" box, hold dumbbells
Full foot on the box — don't just use your toes. Step high enough to challenge you (knee at or above hip when foot is placed)
Press through the heel of the working leg to stand. Don't push off the trailing leg on the ground
Drive the free knee up toward your chest at the top — this is the "knee drive" that mimics hiking stride
Step back down with control — the descent is where real single-leg strength is built
Pallof Pressexample
3×12 ea
lbs
Anti-rotation core — protects spine during shoveling, ski turns, and bike hits
Set cable at chest height. Stand sideways to the machine, feet shoulder-width, slight squat
Hold the handle at your sternum. Brace hard — then press straight out in front of you
The challenge is resisting rotation toward the cable. Your hips and shoulders stay square throughout
Hold extended 1–2 sec, return slowly. Don't let the cable yank you back
If you're rotating at all, the weight is too heavy
Dead Bugexample
3×8 ea
bodyweight
Deep core stability — counters anterior pelvic tilt from desk sitting
Lie on your back. Press your lower back FLAT into the floor — maintain this the entire set. This is the whole point
Arms straight up, knees at 90° above hips (tabletop). Exhale fully before each rep
Extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously — move slowly and with control
The moment your lower back lifts off the floor, that's your range of motion limit for now. Don't go further
Return under control — don't let limbs crash back to start
Optional Conditioning Finisher · 8–10 min

Ski/Bike Intervals: 6 rounds — 30 sec bike erg or ski erg sprint at max effort / 30 sec rest. Or: 3 min step-ups on the stair machine at a challenging pace. Skip this in-season when you're getting plenty of sport-specific cardio.

B
Pull, Press & Carry
Upper body, posture correction, loaded movement
Warm-Up · ~6 min
Cat-Cow + Thoracic Rotationexample
1 min
bodyweight
Undoes forward hunch from desk work; frees thoracic spine for overhead movement
On hands and knees, wrists under shoulders, knees under hips
Cat: exhale, round your entire spine toward the ceiling like an angry cat. Tuck chin and tailbone
Cow: inhale, let belly drop, lift chest and tailbone. Don't just move your lower back — get your whole spine involved
For thoracic rotation: from the hands-and-knees position, place one hand behind your head and rotate that elbow toward the ceiling. Follow with your eyes
Band Pull-Apartsexample
3×15
band
Activates rear delts and mid-traps — posture muscles dormant from sitting
Hold band at shoulder height with arms straight in front of you, hands slightly wider than shoulders
Pull the band apart by driving elbows back — not by shrugging
Squeeze your shoulder blades together hard at full stretch. Hold briefly
Keep your shoulders down away from your ears the whole time
Dead Hang (bar)
3×20 sec
bodyweight
Decompresses spine, activates lat and grip, prepares shoulders
Hang from a pull-up bar with an overhand grip, arms fully straight
Don't go completely passive — keep a very slight shoulder engagement (pack your shoulder blades down slightly)
Breathe and let your spine lengthen. Feel the traction through your lats and spine
Progress to single-arm hangs over time for grip and shoulder strength
Main Work · ~38 min
Weighted Pull-Up or Lat Pulldown
4×5–6
lbs
Weighted pull-ups if you can do 8+ bodyweight. Critical for hauling a loaded pack
Grip slightly wider than shoulder-width, overhand. Dead hang to start each rep
Initiate by pulling your shoulder blades DOWN and back before bending your elbows — this engages lats, not just biceps
Pull until chin clears bar. No kipping — full control up and down
Lower all the way down slowly (3 count). The eccentric is half the training benefit
For Lat Pulldown: same setup cues — initiate with shoulder blades, pull bar to upper chest
Single-Arm Dumbbell Row
3×10 ea
lbs
Unilateral pulling with core engagement. Hits lats, rhomboids, and rear delt
Brace one hand and knee on a bench. Back flat and parallel to the floor — don't twist
Let the dumbbell hang straight down from your shoulder at the start
Drive your elbow up and back toward your hip — not flared out wide
Squeeze at the top briefly. Don't rotate your torso to get more range — that's cheating
Go heavy here. Most people underload single-arm rows significantly
Dumbbell Z-Press or Seated DB Pressexample
3×8
lbs
Overhead pressing without back support — forces core bracing. Great for shoveling strength
Z-Press: sit on the floor with legs straight in front of you. No back support, no cheating
Start with dumbbells at shoulder height, elbows at ~45°. Brace your core before each press
Press straight up — don't let your back arch to get the weight overhead. Ribs stay down
Tight hip flexors from sitting will make this hard at first — that's feedback, not a reason to avoid it
Seated DB press is a fine substitute if floor position is too uncomfortable
Face Pulls (cable)example
3×15
lbs
Rear delt and external rotator health — non-negotiable for desk workers
Cable at or slightly above face height, rope attachment. Grip ends of rope with thumbs up
Pull the rope toward your face — aim for your nose — while flaring elbows out and slightly up
At full pull, hands should be beside your ears with elbows high. This is the external rotation
Go lighter than you think — this is a health movement, not an ego movement. Slow and controlled always
Farmer's Carryexample
4×40m
lbs
Heavy dumbbells or kettlebells. Grip, core, trap, and loaded gait all at once
Pick up weights with a deadlift, stand tall. Shoulders back and down — don't let them creep up
Brace your core like you're wearing a weight belt. Breathe through your nose if possible
Walk with normal gait — short, controlled steps. Don't waddle or sway
Eyes forward, chin neutral. If your grip fails before your legs, the weight is right
Challenge: go heavier with shorter distance rather than lighter with longer distance
Copenhagen Side Plankexample
3×20s ea
bodyweight
Adductor and hip stability. Keeps you in the fall line instead of getting blown back
Set up in a side plank. Top foot rests on a bench or box — not on the floor
Lift your hips up to form a straight line from head to toe. This is harder than it looks because of the adductor load
Regression: bottom knee down. Progression: both legs straight, top leg elevated high
Don't let your top hip sag or rotate forward. Hips stay stacked and square
Suitcase Deadlift (single side)example
3×8 ea
lbs
Lateral core and hip stability under load. Translates to uneven trail terrain
Dumbbell or kettlebell beside one foot, not in front. Hinge down and grip it like a suitcase handle
The challenge: resist leaning toward the weight as you stand. Keep your spine totally vertical
Brace your obliques on the opposite side — they're what's keeping you upright
Stand fully, then hinge back down. Don't let the weight drift in front of you
Optional Finisher · 5 min

Grip & Carry Circuit: 2 rounds — Farmer's carry 40m + 10 pull-up hang switches + 60 sec rest. Or just add 5 minutes of Zone 2 rowing to build aerobic base for long ski days.

Programming Notes
Loading & Progression
  • Add weight when you can complete all sets with 1–2 reps in reserve
  • Priority movements (squat, RDL, pull-up) — add 5–10 lbs when ready
  • Accessory work — add reps first, then weight
  • Don't chase soreness; you need legs for the mountain
Seasonal Adjustments
  • Ski season (Dec–Apr): Prioritize Day A, skip finishers, reduce volume if skiing 2+ days/week
  • Off-season (May–Nov): Run both days, add conditioning finishers
  • Pre-season (Oct–Nov): Focus on single-leg strength and Bulgarian split squats
Desk Posture Priorities
  • Never skip face pulls or band pull-aparts — these are corrective
  • Hip 90/90 and world's greatest stretch every session, no exceptions
  • Dead hangs decompress what sitting compresses
  • Your yoga helps; this doubles down on it
One-Day-Week Strategy
  • Always do Day A if choosing one session
  • Legs and core are your sport-limiting factors, not pushing strength
  • Add one pull-up and one row set at the end of Day A if you have 10 extra min
  • Consistency over a few months beats perfection sporadically
